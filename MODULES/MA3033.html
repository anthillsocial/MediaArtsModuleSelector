MA3033 Experimental Interface Design

Digital Module

AutumnTerm

Convenor: Tom Keene

Computer interfaces are comprised of messages, icons, spaces, and actions. Seen in this way, we can think of interface design as a process of constructing narratives through and with humans, code, graphics, hardware, network infrastructures, and environments.

These narratives draw on metaphors that might involve the workplace (a desktop), domestic life (a home screen), and social relationships (adding a friend on social media) to signify and mediate the possibilities of interaction. If we closely read and decipher these narratives, what might they tell us about the poetics, powers, and politics of everyday life? By extension, how might we use these insights to reimagine and reconfigure interfaces as novel forms of play, and to facilitate critical reflection on digital culture more broadly?

This module blends theoretical and practical approaches to critical interface design. You will learn about the interdisciplinary practices of media artists, game designers, and researchers who critique and examine the politics and history of interface cultures. In doing so, you will critically engage with established media such as classic text-based adventure games, abstract interactive art installations, database narratives, and the hardware interfaces of electronic music. Throughout the module, you will also develop your multimedia design and coding skills, learning new techniques to explore and construct your own experimental interfaces.

Assessments:

  • Production Paper (50%) 1500 words
  • Final Project (50%) 5-10 mins of user experience
